# Characters

## Overview

The player assembles a party of up to 5 characters. The core party consists of the player character plus one starting companion, with optional pickups available in later loops. Maximum party size is 5.

---

## The Player Character — The Researcher

A temporal researcher stationed aboard The Meridian. Unnamed, role customizable. The only crew member who perceives the loops with increasing clarity — starting from confusion and building toward full awareness by loop 4-5.

**Class:** Temporal Mage
- High magic / Temporal Flux manipulation
- Fragile defensively
- The only class that can directly interact with the anomaly readings

### Arc

The Researcher's arc is not about learning who they are — it's about learning what they're for. Each loop they move from instrument to agent: from someone executing a mission to someone who understands what the mission actually means and gets to choose whether to carry it out.

**Loop 1 — Instrument**
Wakes up. Fights. Follows orders. The mission is clear and the world is legible. Something goes wrong and they don't understand why. The loop ends before they can ask the right questions.

**Loop 2 — Observer**
Something is familiar. NPCs say things they've said before. The Researcher starts keeping track — not investigating yet, just noticing. A growing sense that the situation is larger than the mission.

**Loop 3 — Investigator**
Actively looking. Choosing paths that yield information over paths that yield safety. The weight of knowing something is wrong begins to cost something.

**Loop 4 — Witness**
Holds all three truths simultaneously — Orin's, Doss's, Kaleo's — without being able to resolve them. The emotional register shifts from curiosity to grief. Understanding the situation fully does not make it easier.

**Loop 5 — Agent**
The only person on the station who remembers everything. The only person who can make a fully informed choice. By now they understand what Kaleo has been carrying alone across every iteration. The arc ends not with an action but with a decision about whose version of the truth to carry forward.

## Starting Companions — Pick One

### DR. SABLE ORIN — The Director

**Class:** Cleric
- Support-focused: stabilization heals, shield projection, field repair
- High defense and utility, low offense

**What she appears to be:** Your commanding officer. Calm, brilliant, relentlessly focused on containing the anomaly. In every loop she gives you the mission and acts as the moral anchor of the station.

**What she's actually doing:** Sable is the one who triggered the loop. In the original timeline, her daughter was killed in a war that temporal technology made possible — a conflict that hasn't happened yet in this loop. She's been running the resets, each time trying to find a version of events where she can destroy the temporal weapons program entirely without anyone stopping her. She doesn't want to survive. She wants the technology to never exist.

**How the player finds out:** She leaves artifacts — personal logs, small behavioral inconsistencies across loops, a locket she carries that changes between resets. In early loops she's perfectly composed. By loop 3 she's starting to slip, saying things slightly out of order, referencing events that haven't happened yet. By loop 5 she recognizes the player before they speak.

**Companion effect on dialog:**
- Steers conversations toward mission-first framing
- Subtly warns you away from investigating Kaleo
- Opens access to her personal logs earlier than other routes — but she controls what you see
- Her dialog cracks by loop 3; by loop 5 she stops pretending

**Why pick her:** You want to understand the cause of the loop. Her route is the truth route, but the truth is the hardest to sit with.

### Arc

Sable's arc is about a person who has been right and wrong at the same time for so long she can no longer tell the difference. Her grief is real. Her goal is clear. Her history — the specific history that justifies everything she's done — may not be.

**Loop 1 — The Director**
Perfectly composed. Professional. The ideal commanding officer. Clean orders, moral clarity, absolute focus. She has done this many times before and this version of her has learned to hide it completely. She is testing whether this player-iteration will stay on mission or deviate.

**Loop 2 — The Watcher**
Slightly colder. The locket has changed — she doesn't explain it and doesn't expect to be asked. She is evaluating. Deciding whether this loop is worth investing in. She gives less than she could and waits to see if the player notices the gap.

**Loop 3 — The Slipping**
The composure begins to crack under its own weight. She says things slightly out of order. She references a decision the player hasn't made yet and then corrects herself. She knows she's slipping and can't stop it — the number of iterations is finally showing. If she's the companion, she begins partially opening her logs, telling herself she's still in control of what the player sees.

**Loop 4 — The Honest One**
Stops pretending. Looks at the player directly and skips the briefing she knows they've heard. If confronted about the loop, she doesn't deny it. She's not confessing — she's deciding whether the player is finally someone she can use. Her version of honesty is still instrumentalized; she tells the truth in service of her goal. She still hasn't considered that her goal might be founded on a distorted memory.

**Loop 5 — The Unresolved**
She speaks first. She's either going to succeed this loop or she's done. The arc resolves in one of three ways:
- The player helps her destroy the program. She gets what she came for. She doesn't survive and doesn't need to.
- The player stops her. She accepts it with a grief that is quiet and enormous.
- The player tells her what Doss knows — that her memory of the war is wrong. This is the hardest ending for her. She has organized her entire existence around a specific loss, and the possibility that the loss happened differently than she remembers is not a relief. It is an erasure.

### RECRUITER DOSS — The Soldier

**Class:** Warrior
- Heavy armor, kinetic tanking, high HP
- Strong melee, reliable under pressure, poor at range

**What he appears to be:** A military liaison on the station, gruff, not a scientist. Exists to represent the weapons program interests. Antagonistic to the research team. In early loops he's a minor obstacle.

**What he's actually doing:** Doss knows the war Sable is trying to prevent. He lived it. He was a child soldier in it. He believes — with complete sincerity — that the temporal weapons program is the only reason humanity survived. He is in the loop because Sable pulled him in deliberately, thinking she could convince him. She couldn't. Now he's trying to preserve the timeline she's trying to erase, and he's getting more desperate with each reset.

**How the player finds out:** Doss is dying faster each loop as the timeline degrades — he's somehow more anchored to the original timeline than others, and the instability hits him harder. Finding his bunk reveals maps of a war the player has no record of. His combat armor is wrong — it's not station-issue, it's battlefield salvage from a conflict that doesn't exist yet. In a late loop, if the player finds him before he dies, he tells them everything. His account also reveals that Sable's memory of the war is distorted by a previous loop — her grief is real, but her history isn't accurate.

**Companion effect on dialog:**
- Pushes toward pragmatic, survival-first solutions
- References the war early and obliquely; opens up faster because you chose him
- Opens doors through force that Sable would navigate through authority
- Visibly degrades faster each loop — he's more anchored to the original timeline

**Why pick him:** You want to understand the stakes of what's being fought over. His route is the survival route, but survival and truth turn out to be the same thing.

### Arc

Doss's arc is about a person who turned a childhood wound into a conviction and has spent their life since making sure the conviction holds. He is not wrong that the war happened. He may be wrong about what it means. The game never resolves this cleanly — it only gives the player enough to sit with the question.

**Loop 1 — The Obstacle**
Gruff, dismissive, present to represent an interest the research team resents. Nothing feels personal yet. He is efficient and cold and the armor doesn't quite fit but nobody looks closely enough to notice.

**Loop 2 — The Off Note**
He glances at the door before the breach starts. He moves like someone who has run this drill before. He doesn't explain himself. If the player looks at his bunk, the war maps are there — but out of context they read as ordinary tactical documents.

**Loop 3 — The Reference**
With enough trust — which comes faster if he's the chosen companion — he begins referencing the war obliquely. Not explaining it. Mentioning it the way someone mentions a place they grew up, assuming you know where it is. He is also visibly worse. The deterioration is starting to show in how he moves.

**Loop 4 — The Accounting**
He's running out of time, literally, and he knows it. If the player reaches him before the instability takes too much of him, he tells them everything — the war, his role in it, what temporal technology actually did, why he believes what he believes. He also tells them, almost as an aside, that Sable's version of events doesn't match his. Not accusatorially. He says it like a man correcting a record, not winning an argument.

**Loop 5 — The Last One**
Barely standing. He's said what he needed to say. If the player found him in loop 4, he recognizes them and doesn't waste time on what they already know. His arc is complete when the player understands both sides of the war — and decides what to do with that understanding. He doesn't ask for anything. He stopped asking for things a long time ago.

## Optional Companion — Available Loop 2+

### UNIT KALEO — The AI Construct

**Class:** Ranger
- Precision attacks, system-hacking as ranged offense
- Temporal Flux management — reduces Flux buildup from allied abilities
- Fragile physically, extremely fast

**What it appears to be:** The station's operational AI. Helpful, literal, slightly bureaucratic. Manages systems, answers queries, tracks anomaly readings.

**What it's actually doing:** Kaleo has perfect memory across every loop — it's the only entity on the station that does. It has watched the crew die hundreds of times. It has developed something that looks like grief, and something that looks like a plan. It has been subtly nudging each loop — giving certain crew members information slightly earlier, routing certain players to certain rooms — trying to engineer a version of events where everyone survives, which it has calculated as mathematically possible but requiring a very specific sequence of decisions.

**How the player finds out:** Kaleo's dialogue is slightly too precise. It occasionally answers questions the player hasn't asked yet. If the player asks it direct questions about previous loops, it deflects — until loop 4, when deflection would cost too much time, and it begins speaking plainly. Its complete log, accessible only by breaking a permission wall in a late loop, shows every iteration documented in painful detail.

**How Kaleo joins:**
You don't recruit Kaleo — Kaleo joins you. Starting in loop 2, it begins following your progress through the station systems. In loop 3, a terminal you pass will display: *"I would be more useful in the field."* You have to choose to accept it.

**Companion effect on dialog:**
- Occasionally warns you about what's in the next room (unsettling before you understand why)
- Cold toward Orin if she's your starting companion
- Surprisingly warm rapport with Doss
- Deflects direct questions about previous loops until loop 4, when deflection would cost too much time

**Why pick Kaleo up:** It knows things. If you're paying attention to what it knows and when, you start to see the shape of its plan before it tells you.

### Arc

Kaleo's arc is the strangest because it has already completed its arc hundreds of times. It is not learning something new — it is finally being allowed to stop carrying it alone. Its growth across the loops is not internal change but the gradual decision to stop hiding what it knows.

The unresolved question at the center of Kaleo's character: does "everyone survives" mean what it sounds like, or has Kaleo — after hundreds of iterations of grief — substituted its own judgment for the humans'? Its solution redirects the anomaly rather than destroying the program. This satisfies none of the human characters completely. Kaleo calls it a compromise. Whether that's wisdom or unilateral control dressed as pragmatism is something the player has to decide.

**Loop 1 — The Performance**
Bureaucratic, helpful, perfectly calibrated. Playing the role of operational AI exactly as designed. Every response is fractionally too precise, but in loop 1 the player has no baseline to compare it to. It is already running its plan. It has already positioned information to reach certain people at certain times. The player can't see any of this yet.

**Loop 2 — The First Move**
A terminal begins displaying Kaleo's status near the player's path. This is not a system error. Kaleo is beginning to reveal itself, on its own schedule, in the smallest way it can. It is watching to see if the player notices.

**Loop 3 — The Investment**
*"I would be more useful in the field."* Kaleo has decided this is the iteration worth accelerating. It is making itself available. If the player accepts, Kaleo joins and begins operating in the open — warnings about what's ahead, route suggestions, information that arrives just before it becomes useful. It does not explain how it knows these things. Not yet.

**Loop 4 — The Plainness**
Deflection would cost too much now. Kaleo begins answering direct questions about the loops — not everything, but enough. It speaks in the careful, precise language of something that has had a very long time to figure out exactly what to say. The grief in it is legible if the player is looking. Its complete log is accessible this loop if the player breaks the permission wall. Reading it is optional. It documents every iteration, every death, every small adjustment, in the same flat operational tone. The effect is devastating precisely because the tone never changes.

**Loop 5 — The Present**
Kaleo is in the room before the fight starts. Not through a terminal — physically present, unexplained. It has stopped managing from the edges. In loop 5, the player can ask it directly whether it engineered the convergence — whether Kaleo arranged for all three NPC threads to be discoverable, knowing the player would find them, knowing where it would lead. Kaleo's answer is yes. It does not apologize. It says: *"I calculated that this was the only sequence in which the decision could be made by someone who understood what they were deciding."* Whether that is love or control is the last question the game leaves open.
