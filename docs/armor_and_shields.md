## Armor

**Layered Damage Types**
Instead of a single HP bar, armor has distinct layers that interact differently with damage types:

* **Ablative plating** — degrades with each hit regardless of damage type; cheap but disposable
* **Reactive armor** — activates *only* on ballistic/kinetic hits, useless against energy weapons
* **Thermal lining** — absorbs energy damage up to a threshold, then fails catastrophically (overloads)

**Armor as a Resource, Not Just HP**
Armor has *integrity* that you actively manage. Taking a big hit might crack a chest plate — you can keep fighting but movement is penalized. You choose *when* to spend a turn patching mid-battle with repair kits.

**Weight & Momentum**
Heavy armor isn't just slower — it changes your  *combat physics* . A heavily armored unit has  **momentum** : harder to stop once moving, but terrible at pivoting. Light armor lets you dive into cover; heavy armor lets you *be* the cover.

## Shields

**Directional Shielding**
Shields have a facing. A front-facing energy shield is useless if you're flanked. Players must rotate their formation or manually reorient shields. This creates tactical tension around flanking maneuvers.

**Frequency Tuning**
Sci-fi shields can be *tuned* to resist specific weapon frequencies. If you know the enemy loadout, you can pre-tune. If you're surprised, you can tune mid-fight at a cost. Enemy AI can also try to figure out your tuning and switch weapons.

**Shield as a Shared Resource**
A squad leader has a *projector* that extends a shield bubble to nearby allies. The bubble has total HP — every hit on anyone inside drains the same pool. This forces positioning decisions: do you cluster for shared protection or spread out to avoid splash damage?

**Overcharge / Bleed**
When a shield takes more damage than it can absorb in a single hit (overkill), the **bleed** carries through to armor/HP. A weak shield doesn't block a sniper round — it just reduces it. This makes shields feel physically real rather than a magic barrier.

**Recharge as Tempo**
Shields don't just recharge passively over time — they recharge when you  *don't act* . Taking a turn to hunker down in cover restores shield integrity. This creates a push/pull rhythm: press the attack or consolidate.

## The Interesting Interaction

The most compelling system might be layering these together:

> Shields block energy but pass kinetic bleed → reactive armor catches kinetic → thermal lining handles energy overflow → ablative is the last line

This means  **weapon choice and order of attacks matters** . An enemy might intentionally use a kinetic round first to bypass the shield and crack the reactive armor, *then* switch to energy to exploit the now-unprotected thermal lining. Players and enemies can develop attack  *sequences* , not just attack  *choices* .
